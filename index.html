FAQs

How does it work?

The Japanese Name Converter uses a combination of simple rules and artificial intelligence to transliterate English characters into the Japanese syllabic characters used for non-Japanese names (called katakana).

For common English names, a simple dictionary lookup is used. For unknown names, I use a variation on the Transformation-Based Learner (TBL) originally invented by Eric Brill.

Basically, given a list of English/Japanese name pairs, the TBL system learns a series of "rules" to apply in order to get the right output. For instance, the first rule learned is to transform all l's into r's, because there is no l/r distinction in Japanese.

This blog post gives more details, for those interested in a technical answer.

Hey, doofus, you messed up my name! My name's Daenerys Targaryen, and your system got the last vowel wrong!

Congratulations, you took high-school Japanese. This web site was not designed for you.

The rule-based classifier sometimes makes mistakes. In my own tests, it had an accuracy of about 95% (on a per-character basic), but your mileage may vary. In any case, always check with a Japanese friend before getting an ill-advised tattoo based on this web site. (And check with your Mom too.)

Transliteration is not easy, especially when you're dealing with a language as orthographically mangled as English. The vowel system is very irregular, and furthermore, many names are ambiguous (is "Jaime" pronounced "JAY-mee" or "HIGH-may"?). Don't expect perfect results, and you won't be disappointed.
